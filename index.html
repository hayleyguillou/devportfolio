Projects

Time Well Spent (2018 - 2020)

This project explores using a new term, Time Well Spent, in order to capture both time management and well-being during the workday. Our initial 1-week experience sampling study (n=40) found that some users' ratings heavily depended on physical and emotional state. Inspired by Cognitive Behavioural Therapy, we ran a 4-week study (n=22) using a mobile application that prompted users to log their feelings and activities hourly. This work is a preliminary effort to create holistic time management software for knowledge workers.

What we learned: Manual reflection on work output and feelings, increases the user's awareness of the impact of feelings during and towards work.

Reimagining Tableau Public Gallery (2018)

In the Designing For People project course, we worked with Tableau to create requirements for an effective gallery prototype for existing Tableau Public visualizations. By conducting an online questionnaire (n=41) and interviews with visualization experts, we identified the main users, created user stories, and explored their needs in the space of visualization and design. We created a working prototype and discovered how users found inspiration using our tool during a task-based user evaluation.

What we learned: The ability to sort, filter, and search for visualizations based on style elements, visualization type, and topic can inspire users to create visualizations that are more complex, detailed, and interactive.

eyeSee: A Multimodal Lookup Tool (2018)

We created a novel technique for jumping to referenced content (citations, figures, and tables) within an academic paper by using an eye tracker and holding the shift key. An early pilot study involved interviews on current reading habits and initial feedback on the tool. Our evaluation was a within-subjects task comparison where we gathered qualitative feedback on the tool. In our report we outline the external validity issues and give possible future study designs.

What we learned: Users reported less reading resumption lag and better understanding of the paper when using the tool.

Sensing Interruptions Using Psycho-Physiological Sensors (2017)

Designed a controlled lab study for collecting EEG and head position data for users to discover if we can detect interruptions. We had individuals wear sensors while they completed as many sudokus as possible in 30 minutes. During this time, the participant was interrupted by pop-ups with trivia facts and by a person asking casual chat questions. This data was later used to create a machine learning classifier to detect when interruptions happened.

What we learned: Interruptions in the workday can be collected less invasively using automatic detection.

Tutorial Activity Levels (2014)

Created a new metric for tutorials, activity level, that takes into account the views, comments, and sentiment of those reviews. Designed a controlled lab study with five selection tasks based on situational personas. Each task involves selecting from a corpus of 32 tutorials that are presented with other novel metrics including list of commands and a graph showing the breakdown of types of comments. Presented a poster at the GRAND-NCE conference in 2014.

What we learned: Users preferred to choose tutorials based what commands were listed and used activity level to determine if older tutorials were worth exploring.
